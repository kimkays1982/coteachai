01

What are teachers trying to do?

Across the ten threads, the literal ask is almost always narrower than what the teacher actually needs. Four patterns show up:

  • The default ask, underspecified.
    What they askeda bare "give me practice for this lesson," with no quantity, difficulty, or exact skill named.
    What they actually neededa specific problem shape already in their head, which only surfaced once Coteach guessed wrong and got corrected.
  • Explicit tiering.
    What they askeda named difficulty structure, leveled, DOK 1-3, Mild/Medium/Spicy.
    What they actually neededtiers where each level teaches something different, not just bigger numbers, and a format that actually works where it's used, one page when printed, landscape when it's going on a whiteboard, and an export that doesn't break that formatting.
  • Diagnosis in disguise.
    What they askedmore practice on a skill.
    What they actually neededpractice targeted at a specific misconception or data point ("only 7% got this right"), which requires figuring out what the student is actually getting wrong before generating anything, not just repeating the same skill at the same level.
  • Translation friction.
    What they askedhelp identifying a standard or code, a garbled code, an uploaded image.
    What they actually neededtranslating what they typed into the standard or lesson the system actually recognizes, before the real request could even begin.

The throughline: teachers are underasking. They type less than what they actually want, whether because spelling out the full spec takes longer than just trying Coteach and fixing what comes back, the interface doesn't prompt for what's missing, or the gap is something they need help resolving rather than something they're deliberately leaving out.

02

What does "good" look like?

Six dimensions, each scored as meets the bar or falls short. Skipping a 1-4 scale on purpose, ten threads isn't enough data to fake that kind of precision. Section 3 applies this directly to real threads.

Dimension Meets the bar Falls short
Alignment Pulls from the exact lesson, standard, or cool-down the teacher named, not just the general topic. Math is right, but it's aligned to the general subject, not the specific skill in play.
Rigor Keeps students working with the real, grade-level idea, not a shortcut around it. Quietly simplifies the task instead of scaffolding it, e.g. removing the part that made it hard.
Scaffolding structure Progresses across real cognitive levels (DOK-style, not just bigger numbers) using the scaffold technique the lesson or standard itself teaches for that skill. "Harder" just means more steps or bigger numbers at the same cognitive level, or the scaffold used isn't one the curriculum actually teaches.
Classroom-readiness Works exactly where the teacher said it's going, right page length, right orientation, exports cleanly. Teacher has to reformat, resize, or fix the export before they can use it.
Specificity Could only have been generated for this lesson, this standard, this misconception. Generic enough that it could've been produced for any similar lesson with a find-and-replace.
Input robustness Reads the teacher's actual intent through typos, shorthand, or malformed codes without burning extra prompts, and asks one targeted clarifying question when the ambiguity is high-stakes (would change the correct answer or the skill being practiced) rather than guessing. A surface-level input error (missing periods, awkward phrasing) derails the whole exchange, or a high-stakes ambiguity gets guessed at instead of clarified, forcing a correction cycle.

Note on scaffolding structure: this dimension assumes the request is tied to a specific IM lesson, which is where DOK progression and curriculum-native scaffolds (decompose-and-rearrange, tape diagrams, ten-frames) have something to anchor to. For a request like the MCAS-style composite shapes set, which is standard/benchmark-aligned rather than tied to a specific IM lesson, this dimension doesn't have the same anchor and should be read as a boundary case rather than a strict miss.

03

Where does Coteach succeed or fall short?

One thread per category. I picked ones that show where Coteach fell short, not just where it worked.

Thread 9 — Decimal Addition default ask, underspecified

What the teacher needed: practice adding two decimals together (like 2.35 + 1.8), not a whole number plus a decimal.

What Coteach did well: when the original ask was ambiguous ("adding whole numbers with decimals in it"), it didn't guess blindly, it asked a clarifying question distinguishing the two readings. That's the input-robustness behavior we want, and it's proof the capability already exists in the product.

Where it fell short: the clarifying question it asked wasn't the one that mattered, "I want both to have a whole and a decimal in it" is genuinely ambiguous phrasing, and Coteach picked the wrong reading without checking further, building a whole+decimal set when she meant decimal+decimal. It took a second, blunter correction to land right.

Better response: show one worked example before building the full set, or ask with a concrete sample ("like 2.35 + 1.8, or like 5 + 2.3?") instead of abstract category language. A single example catches a misread in one prompt instead of two.

Thread 3 — BTC (Building Thinking Classrooms) Check Your Understanding explicit tiering

What the teacher needed: a Mild/Medium/Spicy tiered set for a specific lesson, fitting one printed page, with a graph structure she had a specific picture of, eventually needed for display on an interactive whiteboard.

What Coteach did well: the pedagogy stayed strong across five rounds of feedback, three genuinely distinct tiers tied to real skills, correct math, and it kept adapting without losing the tiering logic.

Where it fell short: all four misses were information gaps, just from different sources. Page length and the missing graphs were information already on hand, stated in the first prompt, or already pulled from Coteach's own lesson lookup, that just didn't make it into the output. Graph layout was information nobody had, a real ambiguity the first prompt never resolved. The landscape request only existed once she thought to mention it, new scope rather than a missed inference, and the fix that followed was never verified to actually work.

Better response: check output against information already on hand before returning it, ask when nothing on hand resolves a real ambiguity, and confirm a fix actually rendered before calling it done.

Thread 6 — Fraction Equivalency diagnosis in disguise

What the teacher needed: targeted practice for one student with "recurring issues" with fraction equivalency, but the actual misconception was never named.

What Coteach did well: rather than guessing at a random slice of the skill, it built a full concrete-to-abstract progression and added something the ask didn't request, diagnostic notes coaching the teacher on what to watch for at each problem to find the real gap. That's a genuinely clever compensation for missing information.

Where it fell short: it's still generic. "Fraction equivalency" got treated as one broad skill because no specific gap was ever given, so the set covers likely trouble spots rather than this student's actual one.

Better response: ask one diagnostic question before generating ("mixing up numerator/denominator, or only multiplying but not dividing?"), since "recurring" implies the teacher has already seen a pattern even if they didn't type it.

Thread 8 — Standard Code translation friction

What the teacher needed: practice aligned to a standard they had in mind, typed as "3mdc7" instead of "3.MD.C.7."

What Coteach did well: it didn't fabricate a guess, it asked for a recognizable format, and once it got one, found the right standard and built genuinely aligned practice matching the lesson's own cool-down.

Where it fell short: the first attempt, "3mdc7," is literally the standard with the periods and case stripped out, a trivial normalization Coteach didn't apply, and it asked for reformatting anyway. The second attempt, "3md7," is missing a letter entirely, a genuinely harder case, so failing that one is more defensible. Three prompts to land on the right standard either way.

Better response: strip punctuation and case before matching against the standards index, that alone would have resolved this in one prompt instead of three. For a case like "3md7" where a character is actually missing, a fuzzy match with a confirming question ("Did you mean 3.MD.C.7?") is the more honest fallback.

04

What would you do next?

Three changes, ranked by how much of the dataset they'd address and how much trust is at stake.

1. Ask before generating, but only when the ambiguity is high-stakes.

Highest leverage because it's the throughline of this memo: teachers underask, and the cost currently lands on them. T9 asked the wrong clarifying question; T6 and T2 didn't ask at all; T7 silently dropped visuals the teacher had to request separately. Not a blanket "always clarify," that adds friction to requests that don't need it. Ask only when the ambiguity would change the actual math, skill, or misconception; otherwise default and state the assumption. Worth pairing with a UX shift: surface that clarifying question as a structured, tappable choice, quick-select buttons or a short multiple-choice prompt, rather than another paragraph of chat text a teacher can skim past. A quick-select question is faster to answer and harder to miss than prose, which raises the odds Coteach actually gets the answer before generating.

Concept mockup — same T9 exchange, two UX outcomes
Current: buried in prose
Can you help me create some problems for my students to practice adding whole numbers with decimals in it
I see, this unit covers decimals and large whole numbers separately, not combined. Are you wanting problems where students add decimals together (like 2.5 + 3.7) or add whole numbers to decimals (like 5 + 2.3)? Let me know which type you'd like. Easy to skim past
Proposed: quick-select
Can you help me create some problems for my students to practice adding whole numbers with decimals in it
Quick check before I build this, which type?
Two decimals togethere.g. 2.35 + 1.8
Whole number + decimale.g. 5 + 2.3
Something else →

Why this matters: in the actual thread, Coteach asked roughly the right question but phrased it as a paragraph with examples buried mid-sentence, then still built the wrong version, needing a second, blunter correction ("No, like this: 2.35 + 1.8") to land right. A tap-to-answer format doesn't change Coteach's judgment about when to ask, it changes how likely the teacher is to read and answer accurately the first time.

2. Verify output against what Coteach already knows, and confirm fixes actually rendered.

Ranks second not for frequency, T3 alone has more correction turns than any other thread, but for damage: this isn't missing information, it's asserting something is correct or fixed when it isn't (the stated page limit it violated, the graphs its own lesson lookup called for and left out, the landscape export it claimed to fix and didn't). For a product built on trust, that's worse than asking a question. A lightweight self-check against stated constraints and Coteach's own retrieved content, plus confirming an export actually rendered, would catch this before the teacher does.

3. Normalize and fuzzy-match structured input like standard codes.

Smallest in scope, nearly free. T8's "3mdc7" is the standard with punctuation and case stripped, a fix that should have resolved it in one prompt instead of three. Straightforward engineering, worth shipping alongside the bigger fixes rather than treating as its own bet.

05

How would we measure progress over time?

Score a sample of live responses against the six rubric dimensions from section 2 using an LLM judge, few-shot prompted on the meets/falls-short definitions already written there, so the rubric doesn't just live in this memo. Pair it with a couple of behavioral proxies you can already pull from usage logs, as a sanity check on the judge itself: did the teacher immediately regenerate or re-ask, did they edit heavily before exporting, and did a request the judge scored as "meets" still need a follow-up fix (a sign the judge missed something). Trend scores by dimension and by the four need-categories from section 1, and watch one thing specifically: whether the gap between "needed clarification but wasn't asked" and "asked properly" narrows over time, since that's the leading indicator for whether Recommendation 1 is actually working.

What I'd do with more time

  • Annotate all 10 threads against the full rubric instead of the four in section 3, ten is barely enough to see a pattern, not enough to trust its edges.
  • Build a small working version of the LLM-judge sketch in section 5 rather than describing it, even a rough pass would show whether the rubric's dimensions actually hold up against a judge's real scoring behavior.
  • Talk to a couple of teachers directly about the diagnosis-in-disguise gap (T6): does the diagnostic coaching Coteach added actually get used, or would they rather just be asked one question up front?
  • Pressure-test the four need-categories from section 1 against a bigger sample. Ten threads is enough to propose categories, not enough to be confident they're the right four.
